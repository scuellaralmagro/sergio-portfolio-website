## Problem

Attrition is expensive — replacing someone costs months of salary and institutional memory.
The task looks like a clean binary classification: predict, from an employee's profile,
whether they'll leave. The interesting part isn't building the model; it's refusing to be
fooled by the metric that makes a bad model look good.

## The data

- **IBM HR Analytics** (Kaggle, a fictional IBM-authored set): **1,470 employees, 35
  attributes** each — pay, tenure, role, satisfaction scores, commute, overtime.
- **Heavily imbalanced:** only **~16% of employees left.** That single fact drives every
  modelling decision, because a model that predicts "nobody leaves" is already ~84% accurate
  while being completely useless.
- Clean data (no missing values), but three constant columns (`EmployeeCount`, `Over18`,
  `StandardHours`) and an ID column carry no signal and are dropped.

## What the EDA showed

Before any modelling, the exploratory analysis surfaced the real drivers of attrition:

- **Overtime** is the strongest single signal — people working overtime leave at **~30%**.
- **Sales Representatives** churn hardest at **~40%**; managers and research directors barely
  move.
- **Younger employees**, **longer commutes**, and **frequent travel** all skew toward leaving.

## Pipeline

An ETL script does the unglamorous work — drop the dead columns, binary-encode two-value
fields (`Gender`, `OverTime`, and the `Attrition` target), one-hot the rest — and writes a
clean processed CSV. Then an 80/20 split, `StandardScaler`, and **`RandomizedSearchCV`** to
tune **five** models: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Gradient Boosting, SVM, and KNN.

## The metric that matters

Every model landed around **85–89% accuracy** — and that number is a trap. With a rare
positive class, accuracy rewards a model for ignoring the very people you're trying to find.
So the real scoreboard is **recall** (of the people who left, how many did we catch?) and
**F1**. On those, **Logistic Regression won**: F1 **0.59**, recall **0.46**, ROC-AUC **0.72**,
edging out Gradient Boosting — and, being far cheaper to train and serve, it's the one that
got deployed (exported with `joblib`, wrapped in a small `predict.py` CLI).

## Outcome & honest limitations

The winning model still only catches **fewer than half** of real leavers — useful as a
triage signal, not a verdict. That's the honest takeaway, and the clear next step: attack the
imbalance directly with **class weights, resampling (SMOTE), and decision-threshold tuning**,
trading a little accuracy for the recall that actually matters here.
