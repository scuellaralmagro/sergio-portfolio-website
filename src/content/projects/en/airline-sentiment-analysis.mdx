## Problem

Airlines field huge volumes of public feedback on Twitter — and when people bother to
tweet at an airline, it skews negative. Turning that noisy stream into a usable signal —
for support triage and reputation tracking — means classifying each tweet's sentiment
reliably, despite sarcasm, domain slang, and a heavily imbalanced set of labels.

## The data

- **Twitter US Airline Sentiment** (Kaggle / CrowdFlower): **14,640 tweets** about six US
  airlines, scraped February 2015 and human-labelled positive, neutral, or negative.
- **Heavily imbalanced** — roughly **63% negative, 21% neutral, 16% positive** — so raw
  accuracy flatters a lazy model, and per-class metrics matter more than a single number.
- Where a tweet was labelled negative, contributors also tagged a *reason*. A quick EDA
  (in its own notebook) shows the pain is overwhelmingly service and delays: **Customer
  Service Issue (2,910)** and **Late Flight (1,665)** dominate, ahead of cancellations and
  lost luggage.

## Approach

- **EDA first.** A separate notebook maps class balance, sentiment per airline, and the
  negative-reason breakdown above — so the modelling choices are grounded in the data.
- **Preprocessing (NLTK).** Lowercase → tokenize → drop punctuation and English stopwords
  → **strip airline handles** (e.g. `@AmericanAir`) so the model keys on sentiment words
  rather than which carrier is mentioned. For the SVM, tokens are additionally
  **lemmatized** to shrink the feature space.
- **Features.** Binary bag-of-words (word presence), the format NLTK's classifiers expect.
- **Three models, compared** on an 80/20 split: a **VADER** lexicon baseline (no training),
  an **NLTK Naive Bayes** classifier, and a **linear SVM** (scikit-learn `SVC` wrapped as
  an NLTK classifier).

## Results

| Model | Features | Held-out accuracy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| VADER (lexicon, untrained) | — | 44.5% |
| Naive Bayes | Bag-of-words | 78.3% |
| **Linear SVM** | Lemmatized bag-of-words | **78.6%** |

Because the classes are imbalanced, the decision leaned on per-class precision/recall and
F1, not just headline accuracy. The pattern held across models: the **negative** majority
class is easy to catch, while **neutral** — the fuzzy middle — is consistently the hardest
to separate. VADER's failure is the instructive one: an off-the-shelf lexicon nominally
"catches" positive tweets (high recall) but with such low precision that it's unusable on
domain text — a baseline, not a solution. The linear SVM edged out Naive Bayes and was
chosen for deployment.

## My role & decisions

Built solo as an applied NLP exercise. The decisions worth calling out: stripping airline
handles so the classifier learns *sentiment* rather than *carrier identity*; benchmarking
a lexicon baseline against two trained models instead of reaching for just one; selecting
the model on per-class F1 under imbalance rather than on a flattering accuracy figure; and
lemmatizing only where it earned its keep (the SVM). The winning model is exported with
`joblib` behind a small CLI (`sentiment_analyzer.py`) that runs the exact training-time
preprocessing on a fresh tweet and returns a label.

## Outcome

An end-to-end, reproducible pipeline — EDA, preprocessing, a three-model bake-off, and a
deployable classifier — that lands a **linear SVM at ~79% accuracy on held-out airline
tweets**, comfortably past the VADER baseline, with the per-class behaviour understood
rather than hidden behind a single number.
